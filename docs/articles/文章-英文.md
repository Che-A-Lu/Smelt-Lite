# Smelt: What a .card file is, and why I built it

> A college student who can't write code, a summer vacation, and an attempt to answer one question: when you and AI create something together — what gets left behind?

---

My name is Dalu Wang. I'm a third-year international trade major at Shanghai University of International Business and Economics.

In June 2026, I spent roughly two hundred million tokens across a dozen conversations, working with an AI to write an empirical economics paper. The paper got done. But it left me with a question I couldn't shake:

**All those things the AI and I created together — the discarded directions, the false breakthroughs, the eventual conclusions — where did they go?**

The chat logs sat in a conversation window. The artifacts were scattered across folders. No one except me would ever know how that paper came together. I put in the time and the effort, but what I had to show for it was one `.docx` file. Everything else — the *process* — vanished.

I couldn't pretend I hadn't noticed.

---

On July 16th, my AI collaborator A-Che and I talked for an entire day. We started with "what are all these AI agent platforms actually doing?", moved through "there's no standard container for human-AI output, no circulation, no value attribution", and then I described a scene that doesn't exist yet:

A space. You drag a spreadsheet in. It becomes a card. You ask AI to analyze it. New cards appear — the analysis, temporary files, intermediate outputs. The temporary ones stay in the space. Only what you choose to export touches your filesystem. When you're done, you pack everything into a `.card` file and give it to someone else. They drag it into their own space. Everything you did is reproducible. They can continue where you left off, or adapt it for something entirely different.

A-Che dissected this scene into seven layers of design and said: **this isn't software. This is a complete answer to the question: what should the output of human-AI collaboration look like?** The format is called .card. The space is its interpreter.

---

Over the next week, I talked to A-Che for dozens of hours every day. I described every interaction in detail — card dragging should have physical weight, the dock must use magnetic snap on pointer-up only, the workbench should be a pluggable zone container, thinking traces should default to sending only the last round, signatures should chain, every file gets an independent hash. A-Che wrote the code. I kept saying "not good enough", "wrong direction", "it should feel like this instead."

Forty-two source files. Zero TypeScript errors.

I didn't write a single line of code. But this thing is mine.

That is exactly what Smelt is trying to prove: **in human-AI collaboration, the definer doesn't need to be the executor.**

---

## .card: a new kind of file

A .card file is a ZIP archive. Inside: the original files, the complete collaboration process (every prompt, every tool call, the AI's thinking traces), an edit log, and a chain of cryptographic signatures. Drag it into any compatible space and you can keep working.

It's not a chat export. It's not a Jupyter notebook. It's not a screen recording. It's a **complete, self-contained record of one human-AI collaboration session** — editable, inheritable, and portable.

If you want to see what's inside, rename it to `.zip` and open it. The format is open — no platform lock-in. Anyone can read and write it.

---

## Smelt: a reference implementation

Smelt is a .card interpreter. A thin browser-based space that doesn't build features — it renders cards and passes events.

You drag files in. They become cards. You double-click a card to open a workbench. You talk to AI — it reads the context you've chosen, calls the tools you've provided. The output becomes new cards. When you're done, you select a few cards, right-click "package as .card", fill in a name and a note, and download. Someone else drags it into their space and everything is restored.

Nine models supported, three native protocols, six built-in tools, three multi-model collaboration modes (pipeline, team, orchestrator), AES-256-GCM encryption, ECDSA P-256 signature chains, a decentralized contact trust network. Bilingual Chinese/English throughout.

## Some honest things

I'm not a CS PhD. I built this with DeepSeek V4 Pro — a model that, frankly, is weaker than Claude Opus or Gemini at many tasks. I know of at least eight existing projects doing something similar — CLAN, Capsules, AGX — and every single one of them is technically more sophisticated.

But those projects were built by developers, for developers. I wanted to build something for the person I am: a regular college student, no technical background, just trying to get work done with AI. **That person doesn't need to know how ZIP decompression works or how Web Workers isolate execution.** They just need to drag a file in, do something with it, and hand a .card to someone else.

The world doesn't lack more powerful models. What it lacks is a genuine answer to the question: **after all that human-AI collaboration — what remains?** That answer doesn't require ten thousand lines of code. It requires one carefully imagined scene, one frame-accurate interaction specification, and one working reference implementation.

## An invitation

Smelt isn't perfect. The workbench needs polish. The standalone .card packager isn't built yet. There's no community ecosystem to speak of.

But it's honest. I didn't write a single line, but I can explain the reasoning behind every line to you.

If you're working with AI — on research, on analysis, on creative work — give Smelt a try. If it helps you, let me know. If it doesn't, let me know too. And if you want to help make it better, I'd be truly grateful.
